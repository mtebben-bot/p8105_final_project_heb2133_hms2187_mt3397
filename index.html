About

Project Objective

Using data from the World Happiness Report (WHR) and Human Freedom Index (HFI), we set out to explore how citizens’ perceived happiness connects to the degree of human freedom in their corresponding countries.

We begin by presenting a broad visual of how happiness and human freedom are distributed on a global scale, noting particularly high and low ranked countries. We use linear analysis to explore the relationship of human freedom and citizen happiness, including the impact of various subtypes of human freedom. Finally, we investigate the story of “Nordic exceptionalism” by offering a closer look at Nordic countries and how human freedoms may play a role in this narrative.

About our Data

The World Happiness Report

The WHR contains survey results from participants in 156 countries on their subjective wellbeing - in other words, how happy citizens perceive themselves to be. It explores how citizen wellbeing connects to, and can be explained by, various other influencing factors – such as GDP, government, conflicts, and social support. The 2019 report utilizes data from the Gallup World Poll (years 2005 – 2018).

The report and corresponding data can be found at:

  • The 2019 World Happiness Report

The Human Freedom Index (HFI)

The 2019 Human Freedom Index broadly measures the degree of human freedom in 162 countries using various indicators of social and economic freedom including, security, size of government, religion, and civil rights. It then ranks the included countries by their overall “human freedom score”.

The report and corresponding data can be found at:

  • The Human Freedom Index - 2019

HFI Indicators Defined

Human Freedom Score

  • Measure of overall freedom – a composite score based on measures economic and personal freedom

Economic freedom score

  • Composite score based on indicators of economic freedom such as size of government, legal system and property rights, freedom to trade internationally, and regulations.

Personal freedom score

  • Composite score based on indicators of legal protection/security and specific personal freedoms (e.g., movement, religion, assembly, and identity and relationships).

Economic and personal freedom indicators used in these analyses:

Size of government

  • Measure based on government consumption, transfers and subsidies, government investment, top marginal tax rate, and state ownership of assets.

Assembly

  • Freedom of assembly from the Institutional Profiles Database

Association

  • Freedom of association from the Institutional Profiles Database

Movement

  • Based on freedom of domestic movement (freedom to travel within own country), foreign movement (freedom to leave and return to own country), and women’s freedom of movement. Data from CI-RIGHTS Dataset.

Rule of law

  • Composite score based on indicators of whether countries adhere to procedural justice, civil justice, and criminal justice (data from World Justice Project, Rule of Law Index).

Security safety

  • Composite score based on indicators of homicide, disappearances, conflicts, and terrorism, and women’s security and safety (e.g., FGM, inheritance rights).

Homicide

  • A subcomponent of security and safety. Measure based on the UN Office on Drugs and Crime’s data on intentional homicides per 100,000 population. Countries with more homicides receive a lower rating.

Disappearances conflicts and terrorism

  • A subcomponent of security and safety. Composite score based on indicators of disappearances, violent conflicts, organized conflicts, terrorism fatalities, and terrorism injuries.

Inheritance rights

  • A sub-component of women’s security and safety. Measure of whether women and men equal legal rights to inheritance of land and non-land assets

Managing Data

Importing and Cleaning Datasets

library(tidyverse)
library(readxl)

whr_df = 
  read_excel("./data/WHR20_Data.xls") %>% 
  janitor::clean_names() %>% 
  arrange(country_name) %>% 
  rename(countries = country_name) %>% 
  select(-regional_indicator, -standard_error_of_ladder_score, -upperwhisker, -lowerwhisker)

hfi_df = 
  read_excel("./data/human-freedom-index-2019.xlsx",
             skip = 4, na = "-") %>% 
  janitor::clean_names() %>% 
  filter(year == "2017") %>% 
  arrange(countries) %>% 
  select(countries, human_freedom_score, procedural_justice:personal_freedom_score, government_consumption:economic_freedom_score)

Before running any analyses, we needed to import each dataset and do some basic data cleaning. We needed to ensure that we were arranging alphabetically by country and matching the name of the variable for countries in each dataset to successfully merge the data from each index. We selected most variables in each dataset to get a sense of which variables we want to include in the analysis.

Merging Datasets

merged_df = 
  full_join(whr_df, hfi_df, by = "countries") %>% 
  select(countries, ladder_score, human_freedom_score, personal_freedom_score, economic_freedom_score, 
         religion, association, assembly, movement, inheritance_rights, security_safety, 
         disapperances_conflicts_and_terrorism, rule_of_law, homicide, size_of_government)

write.csv(merged_df, "./data/merged_df.csv")

We merged in using a full_join to have all country data from each dataset represented, even if one dataset contains a country but the other does not. For the purpose of the world maps, we wanted to include all countries available for each dataset. For the purpose of other analyses in which we wanted to assess the relationship between World Happiness Scores and Human Freedom Scores, countries that did not have data for both indices were removed from analysis. This merged dataset was exported as a CSV to the data folder and used as the dataset for all other analyses.
